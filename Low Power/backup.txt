\section{Artificial Intelligence stages}
Embedded artificial intelligence can be divided in 3 stages on a consumption basis.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Ultra Low Power, with maximum consumption: 100 $\mu$W.
    Applications:
        \subitem Analog Wake Up. 
    \item Low Power, with maximum consumption: 10 mW.
    Applications:
        \subitem Low Power Microprocessor.
            \subsubitem CC2652R
    \item Normal Power. Applications:
        \subitem High-Performance Embedded systems 
            \subsubitem NVIDIA Jetson.
        
\end{enumerate}
\section{Low-power wake-up use cases}
Give an example by use case.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Detection of a peak event in one signal
    \item Abnormal signal energy detection
    \item Frequency detector
    \item Binary pattern detector : detect the time pattern of successive peak or feature detections in a binary way
    \item Analog pattern detector : detect the analog pattern of features, triggered by an event (peak detection for example) in a analog way
    \item Correlation between 2 signals
\end{itemize}

    \item Logic Gates
    The logic gates consumption is approximately 0.9 $\mu$A for each one. This means that very few could be used on an ultra low power wake up. Needless to say, using discrete components. 
    
    \item Monostables
    Monostables IC consumption is at least 60 $\mu$A. Except for the Texas instruments TPL5111 (System timer for power gating) with a current consumption of 35 nA at 2.5 V. It can be used to enable and disable the power supply for a micro-controller or other system device, drastically reducing the overall system stand-by current during the sleep time. An application for pattern detection is: when the pattern is constant over time, it can activate a wake up every 10 seconds, reducing the average consumption. The other one is the CSS555C from Custom Silicon Solutions, it features an operating current under 5 $\mu$A and a minimum supply voltage of 1.2 V, which leaves it among the most interesting low power monostables.
    %TODO ajouter CSS555C  http://www.customsiliconsolutions.com/downloads/Revised\%20Standard\%20products\/CSS555C\_Spec.pdf
    
    \item Flip FLop 
    The best Flip Flop D found (in terms of consumption) is the SN74HCS72 from Texas Instruments, this one presents a typical $I_{CC}$ of 100 nA. All inputs include Schmitt-triggers, allowing for slow or noisy input signals. The drawback is the necessity of a clock, however clever applications can be developed simulating the clock by another component. 
\end{itemize}
